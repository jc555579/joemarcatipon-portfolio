Beyond the Basics: Why I’m Specializing in Junior Cloud Engineering

By Joemar Catipon • March 2026

When I began my journey as an Information Technology student, my heart was set on backend development. I loved the logical challenge of building algorithms and the satisfaction of creating the core features that drive user experiences. As I gained development experience—building APIs and securing applications through authentication systems—I stumbled upon a world that changed my perspective: The Cloud.

Discovering Efficiency

My initial curiosity sparked when I realized how efficient cloud environments are for modern companies. The vast array of services offered—from computing power to global databases—made me realize that "backend" is just the beginning. I found myself fascinated by the modern way IT infrastructure serves web applications and global users.

Why Cloud Engineering?

The transition to specializing in Cloud Engineering felt natural because it demands a "master of all trades" mindset. To build a truly great system, you must think about:

  • Networking: How data moves securely and quickly across the globe.
  • Databases: How to store and retrieve information at an massive scale.
  • Operating Systems: Managing the environments where code lives and breathes.
  • Security: Protecting the entire infrastructure, not just the code itself.
"In cloud engineering, 'good enough' isn't the goal; 'efficient' is."

The Creative Challenge of Efficiency

What I love most about the cloud is how it forces me to be creative. I am constantly challenged to think about the most cost-effective and performant architecture for a specific situation. It allows me to use every fundamental IT skill I have learned, from networking to development.

My Journey Forward

Currently, I am deep-diving into the AWS ecosystem. Every day, I am improving my understanding of core concepts, services, and networking. By building cloud-native projects, I am not just learning tools—I am learning to architect the future. My goal is to bridge the gap between development and infrastructure to become a cloud engineer who builds systems that are not just functional, but global and resilient.
